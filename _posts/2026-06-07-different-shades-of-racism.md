I didn't realise that racism was still an endemic problem in Europe until I was a teenager, started travelling on my own, especially outside Spain, and saw the world with my own eyes.

## Ignorance is bliss

I spent my childhood in [Telde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telde), a small city on the east coast of Gran Canaria, in the Canary Islands. At school, it wasn't uncommon to be called by a nickname, normally referring to some physical characteristic or, less commonly, to a personality trait[^1]. Some of them were not even based on anything related to the subject. They were probably the outcome of some joke during a conversation, and that's how they stayed.

The thing is, there was always someone we called "blackie", "big head", "giraffe", "blonde", "Swede", "Cuban" or "titan".

Most of us didn't like our nickname. Only a few didn't mind, or so they said, and sometimes they even liked it. I guess it was because, in some weird way, they felt it made them popular.

As for me, my classmates called me “[Moor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moors)” for a couple of years. I didn't really get why, but I always assumed it was because of the colour of my skin or my facial expression. Although in reality I think I look more like someone from Persia or [Hindustan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hindustan).

In any case, I never felt that these nicknames were a form of racial discrimination against me or my classmates. Not even those which, like mine, had to do with skin colour. And funnily enough, I don't recall us ever calling "blackie" our only classmate who actually was it, a girl who had moved from [Senegal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Senegal) to Gran Canaria with her family. These nicknames were more a tool for teasing or mocking than for racism.

The only discrimination I experienced at that age, if it can be called that, was the somewhat dismissive attitude with which some Spaniards from the Iberian Peninsula used to mock the [Canarian accent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canarian_Spanish), just like they did the [Andalusian accent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andalusian_Spanish)[^2].

This "innocence" began to fade when I was around 13 or 14, either before or just after I had started secondary school. These are my earliest memories of hearing certain comments and thinking they were racist, most of them related to the [refugees arriving by small boat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Migrants'_African_routes#Canary_Islands) from sub-Saharan Africa.

Even then, I couldn't understand how anyone would speak that way. Especially since the Canary Islands have historically been a land of migrants. We Canarians have [immigrated to the Americas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canary_Islands#18th_to_19th_century) at various points in our history. And as a strategic point in the Atlantic, we have also welcomed people from all over. For decades, for example, relatively significant communities have settled there permanently, such as the Indian, Korean, Japanese, Chinese and Venezuelan.

Being aware of these expressions of racism made me realise that it's easy to think racism doesn't exist when one doesn't suffer it directly. But even if I acknowledged that racism was still a problem in society, I still saw it as something distant that didn't affect me directly. That is, until I began travelling outside Spain.

## When travelling opens your eyes

The first time I felt discriminated against because of my skin colour was in August 2004, at Newark Airport in [New York](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_City). I was going on holiday with my dad, just over a month before starting my first year at university.

When we reached the border control, my dad, who unlike me fits the white Caucasian man stereotype perfectly, handed over his passport and, after a quick check, went through without any issue. All this despite not speaking a single word of English. I, standing right behind him, wasn't so lucky.

The same officer who had dealt with my father, after checking my passport and looking at me, asked me why I was visiting the United States. I replied, in English, that I was on holiday, pointing to my father, who was waiting a few metres ahead. When he heard me speak, he asked why I was speaking English if my passport was Spanish. The question struck me as absurd, and the only reply I could think of was that I wanted to practise the language. The officer stared at me for a moment and eventually told me I could pass.

Almost three years later, in 2007, while I was on an [Erasmus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erasmus_Programme) exchange in [Prague](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prague), two friends and I decided to visit [Munich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Munich) one weekend in April. We didn't have much money, so we bought tickets for the overnight bus because they were cheaper.

When we reached the German border, the bus was stopped for what, I assumed, was a routine check. Two officers got on and walked right to the back, occasionally pointing a small torch on the passengers' belongings.

When they reached where my friends and I were sitting, they looked at the three of us first, then focused on me and finally asked for my passport. They then told me to wait and got off to do God knows what. They returned after a while, gave me back my documents and told the driver we could continue.

At that moment, I really thought it was a coincidence that, of all the passengers on the bus, they had only asked me for my passport.

Something similar happened in January 2016, when I was travelling on the [TGV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TGV_Lyria) from [Paris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paris) to [Zurich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zurich) after attending [dotSwift](https://web.archive.org/web/20160222104241/ http://www.dotswift.io/), a conference for iOS developers. As we approached the Swiss border, somewhere between [Dijon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dijon) and [Basel](https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basel), some border police officers entered the car where I was sitting and began to walk through it.

When they reached my seat, they looked at me and asked for my documents. After glancing at my Spanish passport, they also asked me to open my rucksack. Having searched it, they returned my passport and wished me good day. If I recall correctly, they only asked one or two other people for their passports. But as far as having their belongings checked, I was the "lucky" one.

The most recent incident of this kind occurred in August 2025. I was travelling by train from Basel to [Freiburg im Breisgau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freiburg_im_Breisgau) with several colleagues to attend a company event. Shortly after leaving Switzerland and entering Germany, two border police officers boarded the car where we were sitting. When they reached my seat, they stopped, looked at me, asked for my passport and, after a brief glance, handed it back and moved on.

Once again, in a carriage full of passengers, I was the only one whose documents were checked. Some of my colleagues were shocked and asked me something like “Does this still happen?”. To which I replied, shrugging: “Yes, but it's fine, I'm used to it by now”.

## Microaggressions

One could think that all these situations were just coincidences. After all, they occurred in a context (border controls) where they would naturally happen. And they really could be; they're nothing more than anecdotes, even if they don't seem like it to me.

Unfortunately, since I moved to Switzerland I've also felt discriminated against in other everyday situations, without any police or border officials involved. I'll give just two examples.

The first happens when I travel on public transport. In Switzerland, it's customary, almost a ritual, that passengers take every other seat when boarding a train, bus or tram, and only choose to sit next to or opposite another person when there's no other free seat. I do it myself all the time.

But now and then, when a white person has to choose between sitting next to me or next to another white person, they usually choose that other person.

As always, the first few times I thought this was just a coincidence. Or perhaps because a woman who preferred to sit next to another woman rather than a man. But then I started to realise that the same thing happens to people with a skin colour similar to or darker than mine. Or, generally speaking, to people who look as though they were "from abroad".

The second instance has to do with the questioning some Swiss people puts me through when we're introduced at a social event. At first, it's just the usual questions we all ask: where are you from, what do you do, etc.

But sooner or later, the moment comes when they ask more in depth about my background or nationality. Because when I reply that I'm Spanish and come from the Canary Islands, the next question is usually, “But were you born there?”. And when I say yes, some still dare to ask, “But are your parents from there too?”.

It's as if they simply cannot believe that someone with my aspect could be an "authentic" Canarian or Spaniard[^3], according to whatever preconceived notion of what it means to be genuinely Canarian or genuinely Spanish, and assuming there's such a thing.

## There's still work to do

All these experiences, however anecdotical or trivial they may seem, are the reason why I believe racism is still a problem.

And I consider myself relatively lucky. I like to think that I don't face more instances of racism because my skin colour is, to a certain extent, ambiguous. It also depends on the region. In Southern Europe, for example, I usually go unnoticed and don't have problems because people who look like me are more common. After all, the Mediterranean has been a melting pot of different cultures and races for millennia.

So I wouldn't dare to generalise and say that, for example, Europe is racist. But equally, I don't believe that the cases we hear about are isolated incidents caused by a minority. A collective mindset shaped over 500 years of history, just to start from European colonialism, cannot be eradicated in three or four generations.

So much so that, in fact, and this is maybe the saddest part of all, having been the victim of racist behaviour has not prevented me from falling into the same trap. It is so deeply ingrained in society that, even unconsciously, we can make gestures or say things that could hurt another person.

Shortly after writing the first draft of this text, I used as a joke with a colleague a term that normally carries a racist meaning. He took it in good humour, but he was also brave enough to take me aside later and explain that, although there was no problem between us, doing that in front of other colleagues could legitimise them to do the same. I apologised immediately and thanked him for pointing it out, trying to hide my embarrassment as best as I could.

Sooner or later, life puts us in our place, and that day I realised, once again, that we still have a long way to go.

[^1]: I learned later that this was, or is, quite common, not only in my city, but also in other parts of Spain, like Andalusia, in the Spanish-speaking Caribbean and other regions of Latin America, like Argentina.

[^2]: Andalusia and the Canary Islands share cultural traits, including the pronunciation of some Spanish phonemes. This is no coincidence: many of the conquistadors and early settlers who inhabited the Canary Islands came from Andalusia and [Extremadura](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extremadura).

[^3]: I have sometimes been tempted to carry a family tree in my wallet showing that, on my mother's side and most of my father's side, all my ancestors going back at least four generations were born in the Canary Islands. Only my paternal grandfather was born in [Valencia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valencian_Community). And my first surname, in fact, originates from France.
